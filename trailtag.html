TrailTag

TrailTag is a personal location device that allows groups on outdoor trips to communicate with each other and send out distress signals. With my small team of developers and designers, I worked on the UX/UI design of the corresponding mobile app for both Android and iOS.

Overview

There are certain risks that come along with outdoor trips, especially in isolated areas. If a member of the group gets separated, it can be extremely difficult to contact or locate them. TrailTag offers people peace of mind by keeping groups connected even without reliable cell service. The group membersâ€™ locations can constantly be viewed by the other members in the app, and distress signals can be sent out to the group if anyone is ever in danger.

While designing the app, it was very important to focus on making it extremely intuitive, since it would be used in active, and potentially stressful or dangerous, situations. Additionally, many people go into nature to remove themselves from technology, so both the app and the personal location device needed to remain unobtrusive—there when you need it, but otherwise wouldn't demand attention.

Research Phase

At the beginning of the project, we had a 3-week research period in which we could familiarize ourselves with the product, conduct user interviews and surveys, decide on the target audience, and determine the scope of the project.

Competitive Audit
 In our research, we wanted to decide which particular use cases would be best to design for due to our time constraints, so I researched competitors in the location-tracking space. TrailTag holds a unique patent, making it stand out from its competitors, but we wanted to find out exactly what things it did better than these other products. I placed the competitors into a few main groups and explained TrailTag's competitive edge.

Main Competitor Groups

  1. Cell-Based: Requires cell service so it can't be used reliably in nature.
  2. Bluetooth connection: (ex. Tile) The range is only about 100ft, so it wouldn't help if someone was separated from their group.
  3. Satellite: Satellite devices are really expensive, don't have reliable service, and aren't made for local group communication.
  4. Off Grid Communication: (ex. GoTenna) This requires the cell phone to be turned on, so it is useless if the phone dies (which may happen in long trips or in cold weather).

Potential Target Customers

Next, we wanted to figure out which activities and sports TrailTag would be most useful for. I researched companies that led guided outdoor group trips and their safety protocol and current methods of communication (if any) to determine if they would benefit from adopting TrailTag. Activities with extremely small group sizes, such as heli skiing and snowmobiling, were not viable target customers because of the negligent risk of group separation.

However, with guided hiking and outdooor expedition trips, the group sizes would often be significantly larger. Sometimes larger groups would split up into smaller groups, each accompanied by a guide. Communication between each of the smaller groups would be highly beneficial in order to coordinate in case of change of plans. In our user interviews, we continued to dig in to see which scenarios TrailTag could be best utilized in.

User Interviews and Surveys
We interviewed many people who participated in outdoor recreational activities, including Northeastern's outdoor club. One member of a Boy Scout Troop shared that his troop limited themselves to mountains with good cell reception because of safety concerns. They also had issues in the past finding groups that got lost because they didn't have good way to locate them quickly. In our interviews with guides of outdoor hiking expeditions, we confirmed that TrailTag would be a good way to communicate between the groups. These guides had PLBs for extreme emergencies in order to notify emergency response teams, but TrailTag could be helpful in preventing the need for a search and rescue altogether. After our interviews, we sent out surveys which confirmed that the high majority of people feel safer when they are able to communicate with the other people in their group.

From our research, we decided to focus on marketing this product to companies and organizations that hold outdoor expeditions and group trips. Therefore, our users would be the guides and participants of these trips.

User Experience

User Flow
Before diving into the UX of the app, I created a user flow diagram to visually show the basic functionality that we would need to include in our designs. In red text, I also noted they ways that the device would work alongside the app.

User Stories
Our team worked using the agile process with weekly sprints. To start off, the user stories we created based on the user flow were put into the Product Backlog. At the beginning of the design phase, our clients prioritized these user stories. Each week we would assign ourselves the next set of user stories to work on.

Paper Wireframes
For each user story, a couple members of our team and I would individually sketch out wireframes. At our meetings, we would compare our sketches and decide what aspects were most successful. Using index cards and pieces of paper, we would then create paper wireframes for each feature.

Balsamiq Wireframes
I would take the paper wireframes and turn them into digital wireframes using Balsamiq. The wireframes were uploaded into Invision and linked with hotspots. We tested and iterated upon this linked prototype before moving into the UI design.

UX Flow
Using the wireframes, I created a document that shows how every screen interacts with one another. The developers used it as a reference while building the app to ensure that the functionality of the code matched the design. Here, I am showing four parts of the much larger flow diagram.

UI Design

Our last phase was styling our wireframes. We did many iterations to find the right blend of colors. Since there were so many different statuses a user could have (guide, in distress, out of range, etc.), it was particularly challenging choosing colors and styles for each of the indicators on the map. We wanted each of them to be easily distinguishable, without becoming too confusing.

Our solution was to utilize three main colors— light blue for guides, teal for regular trip participants, and red for any signs of distress. We kept these colors consistent throughout the app, and used size and shadows to indicate any other changes in status on the map markers.

Material and iOS
We designed a high-fidelity prototype for Android first, following Material Design guidelines so the interactions would be familiar to Android users. After completing that prototype, I translated the designs to be more iOS-friendly and fit the dimensions of the recent iPhone models.
