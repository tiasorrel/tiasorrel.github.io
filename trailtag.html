TrailTag

TrailTag is a personal location device that allows groups on outdoor trips to communicate with each other and send out distress signals. With my small team of developers and designers, I worked on the UX/UI design of the corresponding mobile app for both Android and iOS.

Overview

There are certain risks that come along with outdoor trips, especially in isolated areas. TrailTag offers people peace of mind by keeping groups connected even without reliable cell service. The group membersâ€™ locations can constantly be viewed by the other members in the app, and distress signals can be sent out to the group if anyone is ever in danger.

The app has to be extremely intuitive, since it will be used in active, and potentially stressful situations. Additionally, many people go into nature to remove themselves from technology, so both the app and the personal location device needed to remain unobtrusive—there when you need it, but otherwise wouldn't demand attention.

Research Phase

At the beginning of the project, we had a 3-week research period in which we could familiarize ourselves with the product, conduct user interviews and surveys, decide on the target audience, and determine the scope of the project.

Competitive Audit
 I researched competitors in the location-tracking space to find out exactly what situations TrailTag would have the competitive edge in.

Main Competitor Groups

  1. Cell-Based: Requires cell service so it can't be used reliably in nature.
  2. Bluetooth connection: (ex. Tile) The range is only about 100 ft., so it wouldn't help if someone became separated from their group.
  3. Satellite: Satellite devices are really expensive, don't have reliable service, and aren't made for local group communication.
  4. Off Grid Communication: (ex. GoTenna) This requires the cell phone to be turned on, so it is useless if the phone dies.

Potential Target Customers

Next, we wanted to figure out which activities and sports TrailTag would be most useful for. I researched companies that led guided outdoor group trips and their safety protocol and current methods of communication (if any) to determine if they would benefit from adopting TrailTag.

Guided hiking and outdooor expedition trips tended to have pretty large group sizes. These groups would sometimes split up into smaller groups, each accompanied by a guide. Communication between each of the smaller groups would be highly beneficial in order to coordinate change of plans.

User Interviews and Surveys

  • One member of a Boy Scout Troop shared that his troop limited themselves to mountains with good cell reception for safety reasons. They also had issues in the past finding groups that got lost because they didn't have good way to locate them quickly.
  • Guides of outdoor hiking expeditions had PLBs for extreme emergencies for notifying emergency response teams, but TrailTag could be helpful in preventing the need for a search and rescue altogether.

User Experience

User Flow
I created a user flow diagram to visually show the basic functionality that we would need to include in our designs.

User Stories
Our team worked using the agile process with weekly sprints. We designed the apps based on the prioritization of the user stories.

Paper Wireframes
For each user story, two designers and I would individually sketch out wireframes and decide what aspects of each were most successful. Using index cards, we would then create paper wireframes for each feature.

Balsamiq Wireframes
I turned the paper wireframes into digital wireframes using Balsamiq. We tested and iterated upon this linked prototype through Invision before moving into the UI design.

UX Flow

UI Design

Our last phase was styling our wireframes. Since there were so many different statuses a user could have (guide, in distress, out of range, etc.), it was particularly challenging to choose colors and styles for each of the indicators on the map. We wanted each of them to be easily distinguishable, without becoming too confusing.

Our solution was to utilize three main colors— light blue for guides, teal for regular trip participants, and red for any signs of distress. We kept these colors consistent throughout the app, and used size and shadows to indicate any other changes in status on the map markers.
