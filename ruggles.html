Window to the Neighborhood

For my Experience Design 1 course, we worked on a semester-long project with the challenge of designing a solution to improve our local train station. My design intervention focuses on sharing and broadcasting local events.

Context and Motivation

Ruggles Station is located on Northeastern’s campus, and it bridges “main campus” from the more recent campus expansions along Columbus and Tremont Streets. Despite Northeastern’s push to expand development into the Roxbury side of campus, students still generally feel alienated from the Roxbury neighborhood.

Through our research, we uncovered many high-level social issues regarding gentrification and socioeconomic disparity. Ruggles is both a physical and symbolic divider, but it is also a bridge between communities. I wanted to take the notion of Ruggles as a ‘barrier’ and change it into a connection spot...a window to the neighborhood.

At a Glance

  • Community members would be interested in participating in local events, but are merely unaware of their existence.
  • In my design, event organizers can share their events using a web platform, and this information is then displayed through digital event catalogs throughout Ruggles.
  • This system empowers smaller institutions by providing them with an open and free platform to share their events.
  • These events can also be live-streamed on large screens, allowing passersby and commuters within Ruggles to experience the event remotely.
    • Ruggles Station becomes a dynamic, cultural hub of lively activity.
  • This design encourages people to:
    • Expand their comfort zones
    • Familiarize themselves with the local area
    • Engage with surrounding communities

Physical Displays

Event Catalog Screens
  • On these screens, you are able to find local upcoming events.
    • Ex. a lecture held by a student organization to an art exhibition in Dudley Square.
  • All of these events are a walkable distance from Ruggles station.
  • These screens are located mostly in the central area of the corridor for ease of accessibility.
Broadcasting Screens
  • These screens can show live broadcasts of events happening nearby, or previews of upcoming events.
  • It livens up the space and offers entertainment for people waiting around in the station.
  • It also indicates if it is a live broadcast, so you can perhaps walk over to the event as its happening.
  • Two screens are placed on either end of the corridor to accommodate those who can’t stop to watch the screen for too long.
  • However, for those who are interested, the third screen has a seating area for prolonged viewing.
  • Right now, this space is very under-utilized, so this would bring traffic flow into this otherwise unused space.
Exit Screens
  • These screens are located by the exits.
  • They correspond with the large broadcasting screens to give directions to the event that is being shown.
  • These screens are located mostly in the central area of the corridor for ease of accessibility.
  • Emphasis is placed on the fact that these events are easily accessible by foot and that if an event is happening live, you can be there to see it.
  • Helps to create urgency by showing exactly how much time is left in the event.
  • Arrow on the screen points to which exit should be used to get to the destination the quickest.

Curation System

On the other side is the event organizer’s system. This system, which is hosted on a website, has two key features: submitting an event (the event is added to the event catalog) and scheduling a broadcast (the livestream/preview is displayed on the broadcast screens).

Experience Maps

  • The first experience map depicts the community engagement director at Haitian American Business Expo submitting his event for a Haitian food tasting in Roxbury Crossing.
  • It shows how this system empowers smaller institutions by providing them with an equal platform to share their events.
  • In this scenario, Owen has about a two hour break in between classes. He doesn’t feel like it’s worth it to walk home and back to campus, so he heads over to Ruggles to see what’s going on.
  • He finds out that there is a jazz performance happening locally, and decides to walk there to check it out.
  • This experience shows how this design encourages people to engage in other communities and expand their comfort zones.
  • It makes Ruggles the starting point for a connection and lessens this perception of a divided community.
